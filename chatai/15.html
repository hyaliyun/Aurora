answer:Certainly! Below are some Bayesian statistics exercises that you can work through to reinforce your understanding of Bayesian principles and computation. # Exercise 1: Basic Bayesian Inference Suppose you have a bag containing three coins. One coin is fair (50% chance of heads), the second coin is biased with a 75% chance of heads, and the third coin is biased with a 25% chance of heads. You pick a coin at random and flip it, observing that it lands on heads. 1. What is the prior probability of picking each coin? 2. What is the likelihood of observing heads given each coin? 3. Using Bayes' theorem, calculate the posterior probabilities for each coin. 4. If you flip the same coin again and observe heads, what are the new posterior probabilities? # Exercise 2: Bayesian Updating with Continuous Priors You have a normally distributed prior belief about the mean temperature in May for a certain city. You believe the mean temperature is normally distributed with a mean of 20°C and a standard deviation of 3°C. 1. What is your prior probability density function? 2. You collect a sample of 5 days in May and find the sample mean temperature to be 22°C. Assume the precision (inverse variance) for the likelihood of observing the sample mean is known to be 1. 3. Apply Bayes' theorem to update your beliefs about the mean temperature. Calculate the posterior probability density function. 4. How would your posterior change if you collected a larger sample with the same sample mean? # Exercise 3: Bayesian Hypothesis Testing Imagine you are testing for a rare disease that affects 1 in 10,000 individuals. The test for the disease is 99% sensitive (it detects the disease if it's present 99% of the time) and 99% specific (it correctly identifies no disease for unaffected individuals 99% of the time). 1. If a person tests positive, what is the probability that they actually have the disease? 2. How would the probability change if the prevalence of the disease changes to 1 in 1000 individuals? # Exercise 4: Predictive Distribution Assume you have a dataset for which you believe the outcome variable Y is normally distributed with an unknown mean M and variance 1. M itself is normally distributed with mean 0 and variance 1. You observe a single data point y = 2. 1. Calculate the posterior distribution of M given the observed data point Y = 2. 2. Calculate the predictive distribution of a new observation Yʹ, given that you observed Y = 2. # Exercise 5: Bayesian A/B Testing You are testing two versions of a web page (A and B) to determine which one results in better conversion rates. Initially, you have no information, so you use a uniform prior for the conversion rates of both versions. 1. After the first day, page A was shown to 100 users, and 10 converted. Page B was shown to 100 users, and 15 converted. What are the posterior distributions for the conversion rates of A and B? 2. Calculate the probability that page B is better than page A. 3. If you continue the test, how does additional data affect your posterior beliefs and the probability that page B is better? Remember, Bayesian exercises often involve calculating probabilities or distributions, so having a statistical computing package, such as R or Python with libraries like SciPy or PyMC3, may be useful in solving these problems.
